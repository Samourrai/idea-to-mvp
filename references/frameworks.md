# Entrepreneurship Frameworks Reference

## Durkheim's Sociology Applied to Product & Market Analysis

Émile Durkheim (1858–1917) was the founder of modern sociology. His work on social cohesion, collective norms, and the forces that regulate human behavior offers a powerful diagnostic lens for founders trying to understand *why* a community will or won't adopt a new product.

### The Six Concepts

---

#### 1. Solidarity — *De la division du travail social* (1893)

Durkheim distinguished two types of social cohesion:

**Mechanical solidarity** — found in traditional, homogeneous communities bound by shared values, beliefs, and identity (a craft guild, a religious community, a close-knit ethnic group, a rural profession). In these communities:
- Deviation from norms triggers strong collective disapproval
- Trust is built through shared identity, not demonstrated value
- Change is slow; new products need to feel like they belong to the community
- Word-of-mouth travels through tight networks — one rejection can cascade

**Organic solidarity** — found in modern, differentiated societies where people are interdependent but different (a marketplace, a tech industry, a professional network). In these communities:
- Individuals have more autonomy; deviation is more tolerated
- Trust is built through proven outcomes
- Change is faster; adoption follows demonstrated value
- Word-of-mouth travels through weak ties across diverse networks

**Entrepreneurial implication:** Mechanical solidarity communities require cultural fit above all else. Organic solidarity communities can be convinced by ROI alone. Most communities are a mix — identify which type dominates.

---

#### 2. Conscience Collective — *The Division of Labour* (1893)

The *conscience collective* is the set of shared beliefs, values, and moral attitudes that define what is acceptable, shameful, aspirational, or invisible within a community. It operates as a silent regulator of behavior.

For founders, the key question is: **what does the collective moral code say about this problem?**

| Collective stance on the problem | Entrepreneurial challenge |
|----------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Openly acknowledged and shared | Low friction — build the best solution |
| Privately experienced, not discussed | Need private/anonymous UX; word-of-mouth is weak |
| Stigmatized or shameful | Must destigmatize before selling; long adoption curve |
| Invisible — community doesn't name it yet | Must create the category; high education cost |
| Aspirational — being seen as having this problem is a status signal | Easy sell; social proof accelerates adoption |

**Example:** Mental health tools in cultures where psychological struggle is stigmatized face a conscience collective barrier that UX cannot fix. The product needs a cultural wrapper — reframing the problem as productivity, performance, or wellness — before the core functionality can be sold.

---

#### 3. Faits Sociaux — *The Rules of Sociological Method* (1895)

*Faits sociaux* (social facts) are external forces that constrain individual behavior — laws, professional codes, institutional norms, religious rules, family hierarchies — that exist independently of any individual and exert coercive power.

They are invisible until violated. Most founders discover them through rejection they can't explain.

**Examples in product contexts:**
- A health app that bypasses the doctor-patient relationship violates a social fact in many cultures
- A financial tool that gives women independent control of money violates social facts in patriarchal contexts
- A platform that disintermediates a licensed profession (law, medicine, accounting) violates regulatory and professional social facts
- A tool that makes private family decisions visible to outsiders violates privacy social facts in collectivist cultures

**Diagnostic question:** "Who is *supposed* to handle this problem according to community norms, and what happens to someone who routes around them?"

If the answer involves a licensed professional, a family elder, a religious authority, or a guild — you have a social fact to navigate, not just a UX problem.

---

#### 4. Anomie — *Suicide* (1897)

Anomie is Durkheim's term for the breakdown of social norms that regulate behavior — usually caused by rapid change: industrialization, economic crisis, technological disruption, generational shift, migration, war.

In anomic conditions, people lose their behavioral guideposts. This creates both anxiety and openness. Communities in anomie are:
- More willing to try new products and behaviors
- More anxious — they need the product to provide structure and clarity
- Less able to evaluate options by social proof (the old proof no longer applies)
- More susceptible to products that offer identity anchoring

**Anomie as opportunity:** Many great startups launched into anomic conditions — Airbnb into the 2008 financial crisis (people needed income), Zoom into COVID (offices collapsed overnight), Duolingo into globalization anxiety (people needed new skills). The product provided a new norm when the old one failed.

**Warning:** Building into low-anomie, high-stability communities means fighting deep inertia. Adoption will be slow; switching costs are social as much as functional. Plan accordingly.

---

#### 5. Le Sacré et le Profane — *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912)

Durkheim divided the social world into the **sacred** (things set apart, revered, protected from ordinary contact) and the **profane** (the everyday, the ordinary, the transactional).

Every community has its sacred things — and products that accidentally treat the sacred as profane face visceral, often inarticulate rejection.

**Common sacred things in product contexts:**
- The doctor-patient relationship (sacred in most cultures — monetizing it feels wrong)
- Artisanal craft identity (treating a craftsperson's work as a commodity)
- Family decision-making authority (a product that routes around the patriarch or matriarch)
- Religious practice (a tool that monetizes or gamifies spiritual activity)
- Community solidarity (a product that puts a price on mutual aid)
- Data privacy (in some cultures, personal data has sacred status)

**Diagnostic question:** "Does your product reduce something sacred to a transaction, or bring something private into public view?"

If yes — the product can still succeed, but it needs a ritual wrapper. It needs to be framed, priced, and designed in a way that honors the sacred dimension rather than violating it.

---

#### 6. Social Identity and Collective Membership

Durkheim's broader framework implies that individuals define themselves through collective membership. Adopting a product is always also a social statement — it signals something about which group the user belongs to or aspires to.

**The adoption identity question:** Would using this product mark someone as in-group or out-group in their community?

| Identity signal | Effect on adoption |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| In-group marker (this is what people like us do) | Strong pull; social proof accelerates |
| Aspirational signal (this is what successful people like us do) | Can pull upward mobility motivations |
| Out-group marker (only outsiders / elites / foreigners use this) | Strong barrier; requires identity reframing |
| Status threat (using this makes you look desperate or incompetent) | Kills adoption regardless of product quality |
| Invisible / neutral | No identity friction; adoption is purely functional |

**Design implication:** Early adopter selection matters. The first users of a product define its identity signal for the community. Choosing the right early adopters — people who are respected in-group members — can transform the identity signal from foreign to aspirational.

---

### Applying Durkheim: The Cultural Risk Assessment

Use these five questions as a rapid diagnostic before Phase 2:

1. **Solidarity**: Is this a mechanical or organic solidarity community? How resistant to change?
2. **Conscience collective**: Is the problem legitimate, stigmatized, or invisible?
3. **Faits sociaux**: Whose authority or role does this product bypass?
4. **Anomie**: Is this community disrupted and searching, or stable and resistant?
5. **Sacred**: What does this product risk commoditizing or exposing?

Score cultural fit: 🟢 no significant barriers | 🟡 friction to design around | 🔴 requires social change before product change

## The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

Core insight: Your mom will lie to you about your idea to protect your feelings. So will most people.
The Mom Test says: ask about their life, not your idea.

**Bad questions (idea-centric):**
- "Would you use this?"
- "Do you think this is a good idea?"
- "Would you pay for this?"

**Good questions (life-centric):**
- "Walk me through the last time this happened to you."
- "How do you currently deal with it?"
- "What have you tried before?"
- "How much does this cost you [time, money, frustration] right now?"

**The three rules:**
1. Talk about their life, not your idea.
2. Ask about specific past events, not hypothetical futures.
3. Talk less. Listen more.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

Core insight: People don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job.
The "job" is the progress the customer is trying to make in their life.

**The JTBD question:**
> "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

**Example:** When I'm trying to grow my newsletter audience, I want an easy way to manage paid subscribers, so I can stop worrying about invoicing and focus on writing.

**Why it matters:** Two products can look completely different but be competing for the same job. A milkshake and a banana are both "hired" for the morning commute job: something to eat that occupies one hand while driving.

**Use it to:** Understand what job your product is being hired for. Then design everything — marketing, UX, pricing — around that job.

## Lean Canvas (Ash Maurya, simplified)

A one-page business plan. Fill these 9 boxes:

| Box | Question |
|-----|----------|
| Problem | Top 3 problems you're solving |
| Customer Segments | Who has these problems (be specific) |
| Unique Value Proposition | Why you're different and worth attention |
| Solution | Top 3 features to address the problems |
| Channels | How you'll reach customers |
| Revenue Streams | How you'll make money |
| Cost Structure | What it costs to build and operate |
| Key Metrics | One number you'll watch to know it's working |
| Unfair Advantage | Something you have that can't be easily copied |

**Use it when:** You need to force structure on a chaotic set of ideas. Fill it in pencil — it changes constantly.

## The Painkiller vs. Vitamin Framework

**Vitamins:** Nice to have. People say they want them. They don't consistently take them.
- Low urgency. Competitive market. Hard to retain users.
- Example: A journaling app. A meditation tool. A habit tracker.

**Painkillers:** Solve an immediate, urgent problem. People actively seek relief.
- High urgency. Customers come to you. Easy to price.
- Example: Accounting software during tax season. A tool that prevents your website from going down.

**Use it when:** Pressure-testing whether an idea is a vitamin or painkiller. Ask: "Would someone use this at 2am when they're stressed?" If yes → painkiller. If no → vitamin.

Note: Vitamins can become businesses, but they require much more marketing and behavior change. Painkillers sell themselves.

## The Riskiest Assumption Test

Every business idea has a "kill condition" — one assumption that, if false, means the whole idea collapses.

**Find it by asking:**
> "What has to be true for this business to work?"

List everything. Then ask:
> "Which of these, if false, would kill the business?"

That's your riskiest assumption. Test it first, before anything else.

**Examples:**
- "People will pay a premium for this" → Test: Can you actually charge that price?
- "Customers will switch from [incumbent]" → Test: Can you get one customer to switch?
- "This can be done profitably at scale" → Test: What are your unit economics right now?

## The Hook Model (Nir Eyal, *Hooked*)

Habit-forming products cycle users through four phases repeatedly until the behavior becomes automatic.

### The full loop

```
Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment → [next Trigger]
```

### Trigger types

**External triggers** tell the user what to do next via explicit cues:
- Paid (ads, sponsored content)
- Earned (PR, viral sharing)
- Relationship (word of mouth, referrals)
- Owned (app icon, email list, push notifications)

**Internal triggers** are associations stored in memory — emotions or contexts that automatically cue the behavior. These are the goal.
- Boredom → Instagram, YouTube
- Loneliness → Facebook, WhatsApp
- Uncertainty → Google
- Fear of missing out → Twitter, news apps

Design question: what negative emotion or unresolved tension does your product relieve?

### Action — the Fogg Behavior Model

Action = f(Motivation, Ability, Trigger)

Motivation is hard to increase. Ability is easy to increase by reducing friction. Focus there.

Six ability factors (reduce each to minimum):
1. Time — how long does the action take?
2. Money — what does it cost?
3. Physical effort — how many taps/clicks/steps?
4. Brain cycles — how much thinking is required?
5. Social deviance — does it feel weird or embarrassing?
6. Non-routine — does it require breaking existing habits?

### Variable Reward — three types

| Type | Drives | Examples |
|------|--------|---------|
| Tribe | Social validation, belonging, competition | Likes, comments, follower counts, leaderboards |
| Hunt | Information, resources, deals | News feeds, search results, price alerts |
| Self | Mastery, completion, consistency | Streaks, levels, progress bars, personal records |

**Variable** is critical: finite, predictable rewards lose power quickly. Introduce variability (new content, surprise features, unpredictable social responses) to sustain engagement.

### Investment — what users put in

Investment creates switching costs and loads the next trigger. Types:
- **Data**: history, preferences, personalization settings
- **Content**: posts, playlists, documents, notes
- **Followers/network**: social connections, reputation
- **Skill**: learned workflows, keyboard shortcuts, system familiarity

The more invested a user is, the more valuable the product becomes — and the harder it is to replace with a competitor.

### When NOT to use the Hook Model

The Hook Model is for frequent-use products. If your product is used:
- Monthly → Focus on onboarding and trigger design, skip habit loop
- Yearly → Focus entirely on Investment (so they return) and Trigger (so they remember you)
- Once → Retention is not your problem; acquisition and word-of-mouth are

Forcing habit design onto a low-frequency product creates dark patterns, not habits.

### The Manipulation Matrix (ethical check)

Two questions:
1. Will you use this product yourself?
2. Does it materially improve users' lives?

Use both → **Facilitator** — build it
Use it, doesn't improve → **Entertainer** — acceptable, but know what you're optimizing
Don't use it, does improve → **Peddler** — risky; you lack empathy with the user
Neither → **Dealer** — exploitative; don't build it

## The Traction Channels (Gabriel Weinberg)

19 ways to get customers. Early-stage founders should run cheap tests across 2-3 channels, double down on 1.

Most relevant for ideation-stage founders:
- **Content marketing** — Blog, YouTube, newsletter around the problem space
- **Community building** — Find where your customers already gather; contribute first
- **Direct sales** — Email or call potential customers directly (most underrated for B2B)
- **Viral/referral** — Build sharing into the product (hard to force, but powerful)
- **Partnerships** — Find a business that already serves your customers
- **PR/social** — Share your story of building; document the journey
